================================================================================
  AGRI-SENSE — DEMO SCRIPT
  MVP Crop Recommendation System for Vietnamese Farmers
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
Agri-Sense is an end-to-end crop recommendation system. A user clicks a
location on an interactive map of Vietnam, selects a planting season, and
receives the top 3 recommended crops — each with a predicted yield, a
confidence score, price forecast, fertiliser plan, and farming guidance.

The system pulls real-time data from three public satellite and climate APIs,
feeds it through a machine learning pipeline, and serves everything through a
FastAPI backend that the Next.js frontend consumes.

This script walks through the demo screen by screen, explaining what you see,
where the data comes from, and how the numbers are produced.


================================================================================
PART 1 — THE DASHBOARD (main page)
================================================================================

WHAT YOU SEE
------------
When you first open the app at http://localhost:3000, you land on the
Dashboard. The screen is split roughly 60/40 — an interactive map of Vietnam
on the left, and a Recommendation Panel on the right that starts empty.

There is a navigation bar at the top with two links: "Dashboard" (current page)
and "Market Prices" (covered later in Part 3).


================================================================================
PART 2 — THE MAP COMPONENT (VietnamMap)
================================================================================

WHAT YOU SEE
------------
The map is a standard Leaflet.js map (the same library used in OpenStreetMap)
centered on Vietnam at zoom level 6, so you can see the whole country at once.

When the map loads, you will see small circular markers placed on 13 provinces
across the country — covering the Mekong River Delta in the south, the Red
River Delta in the north, the Central Highlands, and key coastal areas. If you
hover over a marker, a popup shows the province name, region, and the dominant
crop grown there.

When you click anywhere on the map, a green dot appears at the exact spot you
clicked, and the recommendation panel on the right activates.

WHAT IS HAPPENING BEHIND THE SCENES
-------------------------------------
1. The map makes one API call on page load: GET /provinces
   This returns the 13 provinces stored in the backend's geo module
   (utils/geo.py). For each province the backend stores: its name in English
   and Vietnamese, which agroecological region it belongs to, its capital
   coordinates for the map pin, and a separate "farm location" coordinate
   (deliberately chosen to be in actual agricultural land, not the city centre,
   because satellite data over urban areas is unreliable).

2. When you click a point on the map, the frontend records the exact latitude
   and longitude of your click. These are passed directly to the
   Recommendation Panel — nothing else happens at this stage.

3. The green dot is just a visual marker drawn in the browser. The system does
   not snap your click to a province border; it keeps the exact coordinates you
   clicked, which is important because soil and satellite data are fetched for
   that precise location, not for a provincial average.


================================================================================
PART 3 — THE RECOMMENDATION PANEL (RecommendPanel)
================================================================================

WHAT YOU SEE
------------
After clicking the map, the panel on the right comes to life. It has several
sections that are populated as the backend responds.

  A. Season selector and mode toggle
  B. Optional farmer inputs (soil overrides, farm size)
  C. Three crop recommendation cards
  D. Farm plan (if farm size is provided)
  E. Soil health card
  F. Warnings and data freshness indicators

We will go through each one.


--- A. SEASON SELECTOR AND MODE TOGGLE ---

WHAT YOU SEE
------------
At the top of the panel there are two mode buttons: "Today" and "Forecast".

  - "Today" mode uses the current date to figure out which growing season
    Vietnam is currently in, and gives you recommendations for that season.

  - "Forecast" mode lets you pick a target month (1–12) from a dropdown.
    This is useful for planning ahead — e.g., "What should I plant if I want
    to start in March?"

WHAT IS HAPPENING BEHIND THE SCENES
-------------------------------------
Vietnam has three overlapping rice seasons whose dates vary by region. The
backend's seasons module (utils/seasons.py) maps these precisely:

  - Đông Xuân (Winter–Spring): transplant in Oct/Nov, harvest Jan/Feb.
    This is the most productive season — cool, dry weather suits rice.
  - Hè Thu (Summer–Autumn): transplant Apr/May, harvest Jul/Aug.
    Hot and wet; higher pest pressure, lower yields.
  - Mùa (Main/Rainy Season): transplant Jun/Jul, harvest Oct/Nov.
    Traditional season. Lower yield potential.

Not all regions follow all three seasons. The Mekong Delta can do all three
(triple-cropping). The Red River Delta does two. The Central Highlands grows
perennial crops like coffee year-round, so it uses an "annual" window.

When you click Today or pick a forecast month, the backend calls
resolve_season(province_key, ref_date). This function looks at your location
and the date and returns the season name plus a flag if you are within a
14-day "transition window" — i.e., one season is ending and the next is
about to begin. If a transition is detected, the panel will show a second
set of recommendations for the upcoming season alongside the current one.


--- B. FARMER INPUTS (optional) ---

WHAT YOU SEE
------------
Below the season selector are three optional input fields:

  - pH: the acidity of the soil (scale 0–14; neutral is 7)
  - Organic Carbon %: how much decomposed plant matter is in the soil
  - Nitrogen (g/kg): the soil's nitrogen content
  - Farm size (hectares): how large the farm is

These fields come pre-populated with data fetched from a satellite soil
database the moment you clicked the map. But a farmer who has done their own
soil test can override these values.

There is also a Farm Size field. If you fill this in, you get a farm plan that
allocates your land across the three recommended crops.

WHAT IS HAPPENING BEHIND THE SCENES — SOIL DATA
-------------------------------------------------
Soil data comes from SoilGrids 2.0, a global dataset produced by the
International Soil Reference and Information Centre (ISRIC). It is accessed
via a free REST API (rest.isric.org) — no key required.

The backend queries seven soil properties at your exact latitude and longitude:
  - pH in water (phh2o)
  - Soil Organic Carbon (soc)
  - Total Nitrogen
  - Sand, Silt, Clay percentages
  - Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC) — a measure of soil fertility
  - Bulk Density

These are averaged over the top 30 cm of soil (three depth bands: 0–5 cm,
5–15 cm, 15–30 cm) because that is the root zone for most annual crops.

From the sand, silt, and clay percentages the backend calculates a USDA soil
texture class — terms like "sandy loam", "clay loam", "silty clay" — which
is used later as a feature for the machine learning model.

One important edge case: SoilGrids sometimes returns no data for pixels over
rivers, coastlines, or dense urban areas. If the primary pixel is masked, the
backend spiral-searches outward in small steps (up to ±0.02 degrees, roughly
2 km) until it finds valid soil data. This prevents the system from silently
returning zeros or crashing on coastal or deltaic clicks.


--- C. THE THREE CROP RECOMMENDATION CARDS ---

WHAT YOU SEE
------------
This is the heart of the demo. After a moment, three cards appear. Each one
shows:

  - Crop name and an emoji (e.g., "Rice Paddy 🌾")
  - Predicted yield in tonnes per hectare (t/ha)
  - A confidence badge: High (green), Medium (yellow), or Low (red)
  - Expected revenue in Vietnamese Dong (VND) per hectare
  - A 6-month price forecast chart
  - Fertiliser recommendation (NPK quantities and timing)
  - Farming method guidance (land prep, planting, irrigation, pest watch)
  - Estimated harvest window (earliest, latest, optimal dates)

WHAT IS HAPPENING BEHIND THE SCENES — THE FULL PIPELINE
---------------------------------------------------------
When you hit the request button, the frontend sends a POST request to
/recommend with: lat, lon, mode, target month (if forecast), top_k (default 3),
farm size (if entered), and any soil overrides.

The backend then runs through these steps in order:

STEP 1 — FETCH WEATHER DATA (NASA POWER API)
The system calls NASA's POWER (Prediction Of Worldwide Energy Resources) API
at power.larc.nasa.gov. This is a free public API that provides daily weather
data derived from satellite observations — no API key needed.

For the resolved season (e.g., Đông Xuân = Nov 15 to Apr 30), the backend
requests:
  - Daily average, maximum, and minimum air temperature (degrees C)
  - Daily precipitation (mm)
  - Relative humidity (%)
  - Solar radiation — how much sunlight hits the ground (MJ/m²)
  - Wind speed (m/s)

These daily values are then aggregated into season-level features:
  - Total seasonal rainfall (mm)
  - Mean season temperature (°C)
  - Growing Degree Days (GDD) — a measure of accumulated warmth above
    a base temperature of 10°C. Crops need a certain GDD total to mature.
  - Mean solar radiation

Data is cached locally as Parquet files so repeat calls for the same
location and season window are instant.

STEP 2 — FETCH VEGETATION INDEX FROM SATELLITE (Sentinel-2)
The backend queries the Microsoft Planetary Computer, which hosts a free
archive of Sentinel-2 satellite imagery from the European Space Agency.

It searches for cloud-free scenes (less than 40% cloud cover) over your
location within the season window. For each valid scene it:
  1. Reads the red light band (Band 4) and near-infrared band (Band 8).
  2. Computes NDVI = (NIR − Red) / (NIR + Red).
     NDVI (Normalised Difference Vegetation Index) ranges from -1 to +1.
     Bare soil is around 0.1. Healthy dense vegetation is above 0.6.
  3. Masks out pixels that are cloud, shadow, or water (using the satellite's
     Scene Classification Layer) so only vegetation and bare soil pixels count.
  4. Aggregates across all valid pixels to produce: mean NDVI for the season,
     peak NDVI value, and the number of days from planting until NDVI peaks
     (a proxy for crop growth rate).

If no cloud-free scenes exist (e.g., for very early historical dates before
2017), NDVI values are left as "not available" and the model handles the
missing values in the imputation step below.

STEP 3 — ASSEMBLE FEATURES AND HANDLE MISSING VALUES
The backend now has a row of data for your location and season:
  - 8 weather features
  - 3 NDVI features
  - 5 soil features (from SoilGrids)
  - 6 geographic features (which region you're in, elevation zone)
  - Plus one-hot encoded season and soil texture class

Total: 28 features fed into the model.

Missing values are filled using subset-mean imputation. The system groups
historical training data by province and soil texture class, then fills
missing values with the average for that group — not a global average. This
matters because a clay soil in the Mekong Delta behaves very differently from
a clay soil in the Central Highlands.

All numeric features are then scaled to a 0–1 range using the minimum and
maximum values recorded in the training data, which are stored in a file called
scaler_params.json. The same scaling is applied at inference time so the model
sees numbers in the same range as it was trained on.

STEP 4 — CLASSIFY: WHICH CROPS ARE VIABLE HERE?
The backend loads a pre-trained XGBoost Classifier. XGBoost is a gradient
boosting algorithm — it builds many small decision trees in sequence, each one
correcting the errors of the previous one, until the ensemble is accurate.

Given the 28 scaled features, the classifier outputs a probability for each of
the 5 canonical crops:
  - rice_paddy
  - coffee_green
  - cashew_raw
  - pepper_black
  - maize

Example output for a Mekong Delta click in Đông Xuân season:
  rice_paddy: 0.85
  maize: 0.12
  coffee_green: 0.02
  cashew_raw: 0.01
  pepper_black: 0.00

The top 3 by probability are selected as the recommendations. The classifier
was trained on historical yield data from Vietnam's General Statistics Office
(GSO) covering 13 provinces across roughly 4 years.

STEP 5 — REGRESS: HOW MUCH WILL EACH CROP YIELD?
For each of the top 3 crops, a second model — an XGBoost Regressor — predicts
the yield in tonnes per hectare. It takes the same 28 features plus the crop
label itself as input and outputs a single continuous number.

Example: for rice_paddy in that Đông Xuân Mekong click, it might predict
5.2 t/ha. For maize, it might predict 4.1 t/ha.

STEP 6 — CONFIDENCE SCORE
The confidence badge (High / Medium / Low) on each card comes directly from
the classifier probability:
  - High: probability ≥ 0.70
  - Medium: probability 0.50 – 0.70
  - Low: probability < 0.50

If confidence is Low, a yellow warning banner appears at the bottom of the
panel telling the user the model is uncertain and to consult a local agronomist.

STEP 7 — 6-MONTH PRICE FORECAST
Each crop card shows a line chart of expected price over the next 6 months
(in VND per tonne). This comes from the market module (market/price_forecast.py).

The forecast is built in three layers:
  1. A hardcoded baseline price grounded in GSO 2024 averages:
       rice_paddy:   7,200,000 VND/tonne
       coffee_green: 63,000,000 VND/tonne
       cashew_raw:   31,500,000 VND/tonne
       pepper_black: 75,000,000 VND/tonne
       maize:         7,300,000 VND/tonne

  2. Seasonal multipliers: each crop has a 12-month profile of multipliers
     (e.g., rice prices dip during harvest months when supply peaks and rise
     in the lean season before the next harvest).

  3. A supply shock adjustment: if the Open-Meteo 14-day forecast shows
     extreme weather (more than 3 heavy rain days above 10 mm, or more than
     3 days above 32°C), the price forecast gets a small upward nudge because
     supply disruptions tend to push prices up.

Expected revenue per hectare = predicted yield × month-3 forecast price.

STEP 8 — FERTILISER RECOMMENDATION
The fertiliser module (recommendations/fertiliser.py) generates an NPK
(Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium) schedule. The recommended doses are based
on the crop type and adjusted by the soil's current pH and nutrient levels
pulled from SoilGrids.

For example, if soil pH is below 5.5 (acidic), the plan will also recommend
lime application with a specific dosage to correct acidity before planting.
The schedule is broken into application timings (e.g., basal fertiliser
at planting, top-dressing at weeks 3, 6, and 10).

STEP 9 — FARMING METHODS
The farming methods module (recommendations/farming_methods.py) provides
region-specific guidance for land preparation, seed/seedling density, water
management, and pest watch. These are not generated by the ML model — they
are curated lookup tables based on Vietnamese agricultural extension guidelines.

For example, rice in the Mekong Delta gets guidance specific to the triple-crop
system: puddled field preparation, transplanting at a specific density, four
irrigation stages (establishment, active tillering, ripening, maturity), and
alerts for brown planthopper, which is the dominant pest threat in that region.

STEP 10 — HARVEST TIMING
The harvest timing module (recommendations/harvest_timing.py) takes the
planting date (derived from the season window), the crop, and the region, and
outputs a range of expected harvest dates: earliest, latest, and optimal. It
also flags climate risks — flooding risk for Mekong Delta crops, heat stress
for Central Highlands coffee during dry season, and drought risk for coastal
crops in the Central Coast.


--- D. FARM PLAN (if farm size is entered) ---

WHAT YOU SEE
------------
If you typed a farm size (e.g., 2 hectares) into the Farm Size field, a pie
chart appears below the three recommendation cards. It shows how to allocate
your land across the top 3 crops, with hectares and percentages for each slice.
Below the chart is a total expected revenue figure.

WHAT IS HAPPENING BEHIND THE SCENES
-------------------------------------
The farm plan algorithm (recommendations/farm_plan.py) works as follows:

  1. Allocate land in proportion to each crop's classifier probability. A crop
     with 85% probability gets 85% of the total land after the reserve is set
     aside.

  2. Always hold back 10% of the farm area as a replanting buffer in case an
     early crop fails or conditions change.

  3. Weather hedge: if the Open-Meteo 14-day forecast flagged extreme weather,
     the allocation for the top crop is capped at 60% of total area. The
     leftover is redistributed to the second and third crops. This reduces the
     risk of losing everything if the top recommendation fails due to a storm.

  4. Total expected revenue = sum of (allocated hectares × yield × month-3
     price) for all three crops.


--- E. SOIL HEALTH CARD ---

WHAT YOU SEE
------------
At the bottom of the panel, a card summarises the soil at your clicked location.
It shows a health score from 1 to 10, a nutrient status summary (adequate,
deficient, or excess for nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium), and a list of
the top 2 soil issues detected.

WHAT IS HAPPENING BEHIND THE SCENES
-------------------------------------
The soil summary module (recommendations/soil_summary.py) scores the soil
using four signals from SoilGrids:
  - pH: penalises very acidic (< 5.5) or very alkaline (> 8.0) values
  - CEC: higher cation exchange capacity = better nutrient retention = higher score
  - Organic Carbon: low organic matter reduces score
  - Texture: well-draining loam textures score highest; heavy clays and
    pure sands score lower

The top 2 issues are picked from a list that includes: salinity intrusion
(critical in southern Mekong Delta due to seawater tidal push), acid sulfate
soils (widespread in delta lowlands and directly toxic to rice roots),
low organic matter, and slope erosion risk.


--- F. WARNINGS AND DATA FRESHNESS ---

WHAT YOU SEE
------------
A thin strip at the bottom of the panel shows when each data source was last
refreshed — NASA POWER weather is daily, SoilGrids soil data is annual, and
Sentinel-2 NDVI has a 5–14 day lag depending on the satellite's revisit time
and cloud cover.

If any warning flags were raised, they appear in yellow/red banners:
  - Low model confidence (< 0.50)
  - Extreme weather forecast
  - No recent cloud-free satellite image available

These are not blocking errors — the system still returns recommendations — but
they tell the user to interpret results with more caution.


================================================================================
PART 4 — THE MARKET PRICES PAGE
================================================================================

WHAT YOU SEE
------------
Click "Market Prices" in the navigation bar to open this page. At the top is
a dropdown to select one of the 5 canonical crops. Below it you see:

  - A historical price chart: bar chart showing monthly prices for the past
    12 months (default), with an overlay line showing the trend.
  - A forecast chart: the next 6 months, shown as a line with shaded confidence
    bands (upper and lower bounds).
  - Four summary metrics:
      * Volatility Index (a decimal number) and a label: Low, Moderate, or High
      * Best selling months (when prices are historically highest)
      * Months to avoid selling (when prices are historically lowest)
      * Key drivers: a short list of supply and demand factors

WHAT IS HAPPENING BEHIND THE SCENES
-------------------------------------
The frontend calls GET /market/prices?crop=rice_paddy&months_back=12
&months_forward=6. The backend's market module (market/price_forecast.py)
builds the response in three steps:

HISTORICAL PRICES
The baseline price (e.g., 7.2M VND/tonne for rice) is multiplied by monthly
seasonal multipliers for the past 12 months. These multipliers are empirically
grounded in GSO price patterns — for instance, rice prices typically fall 5–8%
in March (main harvest) and recover in August (lean season). The "source" field
on each historical data point is labelled "GSO-derived estimate" to be
transparent that this is a model, not a live price feed.

FORECAST PRICES
The same multiplier logic is applied to future months. If an extreme weather
flag is active (from the Open-Meteo 14-day forecast), a supply shock multiplier
adds a small upward adjustment. The upper and lower confidence bands are set
at ±10% of the forecast price (wider for more volatile crops like pepper).

VOLATILITY INDEX
Volatility is calculated as the standard deviation of the month-over-month
percentage price changes divided by the mean price. A result below 0.08 is
labelled Low, 0.08–0.15 is Moderate, above 0.15 is High. Coffee and pepper
score High because international commodity prices are far more volatile than
domestically-consumed staples like rice.

KEY DRIVERS
These are static, crop-specific text labels — for example, for coffee: "Global
Arabica/Robusta price spread", "Seasonal harvest timing Central Highlands",
"Export demand from Europe and the US". For rice: "Domestic procurement policy
(VFA reserve purchases)", "Regional supply (Mekong Delta triple-crop output)".
These are not generated dynamically; they reflect known structural drivers for
each crop.


================================================================================
PART 5 — SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE SUMMARY
================================================================================

For a complete picture, here is the data flow from click to screen:

  1. USER CLICKS MAP
     → Frontend (Next.js) sends POST /recommend (lat, lon, season, mode)

  2. BACKEND VALIDATES REQUEST
     → Confirms coordinates are within Vietnam's bounding box
     → Resolves season from date and region

  3. DATA FETCHING (three real-time API calls)
     → NASA POWER: daily weather for the season window
     → SoilGrids 2.0: soil properties at the clicked point
     → Planetary Computer / Sentinel-2: NDVI vegetation index

  4. FEATURE ENGINEERING
     → Aggregate daily weather to season totals/averages
     → Compute Growing Degree Days, NDVI peak and timing
     → Fill any missing values using province+texture group means
     → Scale all features to [0, 1] range

  5. MACHINE LEARNING
     → XGBoost Classifier: outputs probability for each of 5 crops
     → XGBoost Regressor: outputs predicted yield (t/ha) per crop
     → Select top 3 crops by probability

  6. BUSINESS LOGIC LAYER
     → Price forecast (seasonal multipliers + weather shock)
     → Fertiliser plan (NPK schedule adjusted for soil pH and nutrients)
     → Farming methods (region-specific cultivation guidance)
     → Harvest timing (planting date → harvest window + climate risks)
     → Farm plan (probability-weighted land allocation + weather hedge)
     → Soil health score (composite of pH, CEC, organic carbon, texture)

  7. RESPONSE ASSEMBLED AND RETURNED
     → Frontend renders three crop cards + soil card + charts

Public data sources used (all free, no API keys):
  - NASA POWER (power.larc.nasa.gov)
  - SoilGrids 2.0 (rest.isric.org)
  - Microsoft Planetary Computer / Sentinel-2
  - Open-Meteo (short-term weather forecast)
  - Vietnam General Statistics Office (GSO) — offline CSV download


================================================================================
KNOWN LIMITATIONS (to mention during demo Q&A)
================================================================================

  - The training dataset has 270 rows covering 13 provinces and 5 crops across
    ~4 years of GSO data. This is small for machine learning. The classifier
    achieves ~81% accuracy and the yield model has an error of ±0.43 t/ha.
    Both will improve significantly with more training data.

  - Prices are model-generated estimates, not live market data. A future version
    would scrape real-time price feeds from provincial commodity exchanges.

  - Sentinel-2 NDVI data has a 5–14 day lag due to satellite revisit time and
    cloud masking. In a cloudy monsoon season, there may be no valid image for
    weeks, in which case NDVI is left missing and the ML model uses imputed
    values.

  - The system covers 5 crops. Vietnam has hundreds of commercially grown
    crops. Expansion to include vegetables, fruit trees, aquaculture, and
    industrial crops is planned for v2.

  - Confidence scores come from the classifier's raw probability output. They
    are not calibrated against observed prediction accuracy, so treat a 0.85
    confidence score as "the model is relatively sure" rather than "this will
    be right 85% of the time."
